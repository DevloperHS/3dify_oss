# Storage Provider Fit: Cloudinary (Source Images) + Cloudflare R2 (GLB Assets)

Scope: confirm, against primary/official sources only, whether Cloudinary's free tier is adequate for storing user-uploaded source images and whether Cloudflare R2 is adequate for storing/serving generated GLB 3D assets, for a production multi-user async image-to-3D web service.

Research date: 2026-07-11

## Summary Table

| Provider | Free Storage | Free Bandwidth/Egress | Relevant Limits | Production-suitable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cloudinary (source images) | 25 credits/month, where 1 credit = 1 GB of storage (shared pool with bandwidth/transformations) | 1 credit = 1 GB delivered bandwidth (rolling 30-day window), also drawn from the same 25-credit pool | Admin API capped at 500 req/hour on Free plan (Upload API is not rate-limited); ToS bars "further commercialization or resale" of the *Cloudinary service itself* and bars building a competing product — does not bar using Cloudinary inside your own commercial product | Caveat — fine for MVP/low volume; the 25-credit/month pool is shared across storage+bandwidth+transformations so it will be the first thing to exhaust as user count grows, requiring an upgrade to a paid plan well before it becomes a technical/legal blocker |
| Cloudflare R2 (GLB assets) | 10 GB-month/month, Standard storage class only | Explicitly $0 — "There are no charges for egress bandwidth for any storage class" | Class A (writes/lists) 1M req/month free; Class B (reads) 10M req/month free; public serving via the free `r2.dev` subdomain is rate-limited and documented as dev-only — production public serving requires attaching a custom domain (still free, just needs a domain on Cloudflare) | Yes — no egress cost regardless of view volume; only storage/operations scale with cost, and R2 is general-purpose object storage with no file-type restriction (GLBs are fine) |

## Cloudinary

**Free plan credits.** Cloudinary's Free plan includes "3 Users / 1 Account" and "25 monthly credits," advertised as "Free forever" with "No credit card required." ([cloudinary.com/pricing](https://cloudinary.com/pricing))

**Credit conversion / shared pool.** Cloudinary's documentation confirms storage, bandwidth, and transformations all draw from one shared credit pool: "You can flexibly utilize the credits in your free or paid plan for transformations, storage and/or bandwidth based on your use case," and "One credit can be used for: 1000 transformations...1 GB of managed storage...1 GB of delivered bandwidth...500 SD video seconds...250 HD video processing seconds." ([cloudinary.com/documentation/developer_onboarding_faq_credits](https://cloudinary.com/documentation/developer_onboarding_faq_credits))

This means the Free plan's effective cap is **25 GB total if used purely for storage**, or a mix of storage + bandwidth + transformations that together must stay under 25 credits/month. For this project, source images are "generally small, one per generation job," so raw storage is unlikely to be the binding constraint — the shared pool (especially delivered bandwidth for repeated previews) will be consumed faster as user/job count grows.

**Measurement windows.** Per the same documentation: "Transformations and bandwidth are measured over a rolling 30-day window... Storage works differently. It reflects your current total at any moment, not a rolling calculation." ([cloudinary.com/documentation/developer_onboarding_faq_credits](https://cloudinary.com/documentation/developer_onboarding_faq_credits))

**API rate limits.** Cloudinary's support documentation states the Admin API (listing/deleting resources) is capped at 500 requests/hour on the Free plan, rising to 2000 requests/hour on paid plans; the Upload API used to actually push new source images is not rate-limited this way. (Source: [support.cloudinary.com — "Are Cloudinary APIs rate-limited?"](https://support.cloudinary.com/hc/en-us/articles/202520892-Are-Cloudinary-APIs-rate-limited) — note: this specific support article returned HTTP 403 on direct fetch during this research session; the figures above come from Cloudinary's own support-site content surfaced via search snippet and are consistent with Cloudinary's publicly documented Admin API rate-limit behavior. Treat this one figure as needing a final manual confirmation by visiting the URL directly in a browser, since it could not be fetched programmatically.)

**Commercial-use language in ToS.** Cloudinary's Terms of Use (Section 2, "Grant of Right") state: "Cloudinary grants you a worldwide, limited, non-exclusive, revocable, non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to use the Services solely for your business purposes for websites and mobile applications, owned or operated by you, **and not for further commercialization or resale**." ([cloudinary.com/tou](https://cloudinary.com/tou))

Read in context, this restricts *reselling the Cloudinary platform itself* (e.g., you cannot resell "image hosting as a service" built directly on a thin Cloudinary wrapper) — it does not prohibit using Cloudinary as infrastructure inside your own different commercial product (an image-to-3D generation service). This reading is reinforced by the Acceptable Use section of the same Terms: "You may not access or use the Services for any illegal or abusive purposes, or to develop or create a similar or competitive product or service to the Services." ([cloudinary.com/tou](https://cloudinary.com/tou))

**Account enforcement.** Cloudinary reserves broad rights to act on abuse or plan-limit circumvention: "Your use of the Account is limited to the scope of use and storage permitted under your subscription plan," and opening "multiple accounts to bypass usage restrictions and/or gain additional storage or usage across a number of accounts is prohibited." Cloudinary "may temporarily or permanently deny, limit, suspend, or terminate your user account without refund, if Cloudinary determines that you abused your rights to use the Services, breached the Terms, violated any applicable law, or performed any act which is harmful or likely to be harmful to others." ([cloudinary.com/trust/aup](https://cloudinary.com/trust/aup), [cloudinary.com/tou](https://cloudinary.com/tou))

**No explicit "no production use" clause found.** Nothing in the pricing page, ToS, or AUP explicitly bars running a production/commercial multi-tenant service on the Free plan — the constraint is purely the 25-credit/month quota and the 500 req/hour Admin API cap, not a contractual prohibition.

## Cloudflare R2

**Zero egress — exact statement.** Cloudflare's R2 pricing page states plainly: "There are no charges for egress bandwidth for any storage class." ([developers.cloudflare.com/r2/pricing](https://developers.cloudflare.com/r2/pricing/)) Cloudflare's R2 product page reinforces this as a core positioning point: "R2 doesn't charge for egress" / "Object storage without egress fees." ([cloudflare.com/products/r2](https://www.cloudflare.com/products/r2/)) This directly confirms the deciding factor cited versus AWS S3, where egress (data-out) is billed per GB.

**Free tier caps.** Per the official pricing table: Storage — **10 GB-month/month** (Standard storage class only: "The free tier only applies to Standard storage, and does not apply to Infrequent Access storage"); Class A operations (writes, lists) — **1,000,000 requests/month**; Class B operations (reads) — **10,000,000 requests/month**. ([developers.cloudflare.com/r2/pricing](https://developers.cloudflare.com/r2/pricing/))

**File-type restrictions — none technical.** The R2 overview page describes R2 as general-purpose storage for "large amounts of unstructured data" with example use cases including "cloud storage for web content," "podcast episodes," and "machine learning model artifacts" — no restriction to images or any particular MIME type. ([developers.cloudflare.com/r2](https://developers.cloudflare.com/r2/)) GLB binary files are unremarkable for this product.

**Content/legal restrictions.** R2 is classified by Cloudflare as a "hosting product" and is therefore subject to Cloudflare's Acceptable Hosting Policy and the Developer Platform section of Cloudflare's Service-Specific Terms — content that is illegal, CSAM, or otherwise harmful can be blocked/removed, but there is no clause restricting *file type* or *format* for legitimate content such as generated 3D assets. A separate clause in Cloudflare's Service-Specific Terms (Content Delivery Network section) reserves the right to disable/limit the **free CDN/proxy** product if it's used "without...Paid Services to serve video or a disproportionate percentage of pictures, audio files, or other large files" — but this targets people using Cloudflare's free website-proxy CDN as a bandwidth-shifting trick for a non-Cloudflare origin, not R2 itself, which is explicitly priced and designed for exactly this file-serving use case. ([cloudflare.com/service-specific-terms-application-services](https://www.cloudflare.com/service-specific-terms-application-services/))

**Public serving requires a domain — and r2.dev is dev-only.** R2 buckets are private by default. To serve files to a browser `<model-viewer>`, you must explicitly enable public access via one of two paths, per Cloudflare's docs ([developers.cloudflare.com/r2/buckets/public-buckets](https://developers.cloudflare.com/r2/buckets/public-buckets/)):
- **Managed `r2.dev` subdomain** — enabled via Settings > Public Development URL. Cloudflare's docs explicitly warn: "Public access through `r2.dev` subdomains is rate-limited and should only be used for development purposes." This is **not suitable for production**.
- **Custom domain** — add your own domain as a Cloudflare zone, then attach it under bucket Settings > Custom Domains. This is the supported production path, is still free, does not require a Cloudflare Worker, and unlocks caching/WAF/access-control features. It does require the project to own/manage a domain on Cloudflare's DNS.

No Cloudflare Worker is required simply to serve public GLB files — a Worker would only be needed for extra logic (e.g., auth-gating downloads, signed URLs, per-request transforms).

## Recommendation

**Cloudinary for Source Images — go, with caveat.** Fine as the source-image store for an MVP and for a while beyond it: source photos are small, and Cloudinary's Free plan gives 25 GB-equivalent credits/month shared across storage + bandwidth + transformations. There is no contractual bar to commercial/production use — the "not for further commercialization or resale" clause targets reselling Cloudinary itself, not using it inside a different product. The real constraint is the shared 25-credit pool: as concurrent users grow, delivered bandwidth (previews of source images in-app) will consume credits alongside storage, and the 500 req/hour Admin API cap (not the Upload API) is a secondary limit to watch if the backend polls/lists Cloudinary resources frequently. Plan to move to a paid Cloudinary plan once monthly active users push combined storage+bandwidth past ~20-25 GB-equivalent — this is a capacity/cost decision, not a ToS blocker.

**Cloudflare R2 for GLB Assets — go, indefinitely for the egress question.** R2 is well-suited to this role specifically because of the zero-egress guarantee: GLB files are "served repeatedly to viewers" and are "larger binary files" (MB to tens of MB), which is exactly the workload that would generate large, unpredictable egress bills on S3. On R2 that cost is structurally zero regardless of view volume. Storage cost still scales linearly past the 10 GB-month free cap (paid Standard storage is billed per GB-month beyond that), and Class A/B operation limits (1M writes, 10M reads per month free) are generous for a single-image-per-job pipeline but should be monitored if the service scales to very high job/view counts. The one required production adjustment: do not rely on the `r2.dev` subdomain to serve GLBs to end users (Cloudflare's own docs call it dev-only and rate-limited) — attach a custom domain to the bucket before launch, which is free but requires the project's domain to be managed on Cloudflare.
